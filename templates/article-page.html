Decision-Making

The Architecture of Decisions

April 8, 2026 · 12 min read · Strategy
NA
Nicholas Ashkar
Founder, Cirvgreen

Every consequential choice exists on a spectrum between fully reversible and completely permanent. The builders who move fastest aren't those who decide quickest — they're those who correctly classify which decisions belong to each end of that spectrum, and then treat them accordingly.

This framework changed how I think about organisational pace. A company that applies the same deliberation process to choosing a button colour as it does to signing a ten-year lease isn't being rigorous — it's being inefficient in a way that compounds over time until speed itself becomes impossible.

Key concept

Jeff Bezos famously separated "one-way door" (irreversible) decisions from "two-way door" (reversible) ones. One-way doors require senior judgment and slow deliberation. Two-way doors should be delegated and made quickly.

First Principles

Start with what you can actually observe. Before classifying a decision, ask two questions: How much does reversing this cost, and by when must you decide? These two variables — reversal cost and time pressure — map almost every decision onto a 2×2 matrix that tells you how much process to apply.

Most organisations, when they teach decision-making, skip this classification step entirely. They go straight to frameworks like pros/cons lists or multi-criteria analysis. The result: overthought low-stakes calls, and underthought high-stakes ones.

"The best decisions I've made were the ones where I correctly identified what I couldn't undo, and spent my energy there rather than everywhere."

Reversible vs. Irreversible

Reversibility is not binary. It's a gradient. A decision to try a new marketing channel can be reversed in three weeks at the cost of one sprint. A decision to build proprietary infrastructure can take three years and $2M to reverse. Both feel like "just picking a direction" in the moment.

The definition of wisdom is knowing what you don't know, and the definition of strategic wisdom is knowing which of those unknowns matter.

— Nassim Nicholas Taleb, paraphrased

A useful heuristic: if reversing this decision requires n > 3 teams, external contracts, or architectural migrations, classify it as irreversible and treat it accordingly. If one person can undo it in an afternoon, move fast.

The Cost of Latency

Decision latency — the time between when a decision should be made and when it actually is — carries a compounding cost that most organisations fail to account for. It's invisible, because the cost appears as "work that didn't happen" rather than money spent. 

// Decision latency model — cost compounds per day
function latencyCost(
  dailyOpportunityCost: number,
  daysDelayed: number,
  compoundRate: number = 0.03
) {
  return dailyOpportunityCost
    * (((1 + compoundRate) ** daysDelayed - 1) / compoundRate);
}

// $500/day decision, delayed 30 days, 3% daily compound:
latencyCost(500, 30); // → ~$23,700

Evidence

Across 200 product decisions I tracked over 18 months at Cirvgreen, a pattern emerged: the decisions that took longest to make were not the ones that were most consequential — they were the ones where the classification was unclear at the start.

Warning

Beware "fake irreversibility" — decisions that feel permanent because they're large or expensive, but are actually reversible with enough will. These consume the most senior attention for the least return.

The 15 decisions that took more than 3 weeks had an average outcome rating of 3.1/5. The 15 that were made in under 48 hours rated 4.2/5 on average. The confounding variable — both groups had similar stakes — was almost entirely about clarity of classification at the outset.

The three most common causes of mis-classification:

  • Status quo bias — treating change as inherently riskier than inaction
  • Sunk cost conflation — confusing "we've invested a lot" with "this is now irreversible"
  • Scope creep in deliberation — expanding what one decision needs to solve

The Counterargument

A reasonable objection: doesn't this framework just encourage speed at the cost of quality? Doesn't moving fast on two-way-door decisions increase the chance of compounding errors — where ten fast wrong decisions create a mess that's harder to reverse than one slow right one?

Tip

Counteract compounding errors with explicit "decision logs" — short notes on what you decided, why, and what would change your mind. They take 5 minutes and make reversal 10x faster.

The counter-counterargument has three parts:

  1. Error detection is faster in rapid iteration — feedback loops are tighter
  2. The cost of mis-classifying a two-way-door decision as one-way is usually higher than the converse
  3. Decision logs (see above) make reversal explicit and cheap
✦ ✦ ✦

Conclusion

Good decision architecture is not about making more decisions faster. It is about applying the right amount of deliberation to the right class of problem. The tool is classification. Everything else — speed, quality, delegation — follows from getting that right.

If you take one thing from this: before your next important decision, stop and spend five minutes answering "what would it take to reverse this, and does that cost justify slowing down?" That question alone will buy back more time than most productivity frameworks combined.
